The Story

From air mattresses
to a $75B empire

How three roommates who couldn't pay rent built one of the world's most valuable companies.

2007
The idea is born
Brian Chesky & Joe Gebbia rent out 3 air mattresses in their SF apartment during a design conference. They earn $1,000 in a few days.
2008
Airbnb officially launches
Nathan Blecharczyk joins as co-founder. The platform launches at the Democratic National Convention. First major test of the model.
2009
Y Combinator changes everything
Accepted into Y Combinator with $20,000 seed funding. The Craigslist growth hack drives viral user acquisition.
2011
Global expansion begins
Raises $112M Series B. Expands to Europe. Acquires Accoleo in Germany. 1 million nights booked milestone.
2014
'Belong Anywhere' rebrand
Launches iconic Bélo logo and emotional brand identity. Revenue hits $250M. Valued at $10B+.
2016
Airbnb Experiences launches
Expands beyond accommodation to curated local activities hosted by locals. Platform stickiness increases dramatically.
2017
First profitable year
Revenue reaches $2.6B. Company turns profitable for the first time. 100 million total guests served.
2019
Pre-IPO peak
Revenue hits $4.8B. Valued at $31B. Files for IPO - then COVID-19 hits.
2020
IPO during a pandemic
Lays off 1,900 staff. Pivots to long-term stays. IPOs at $68/share, surges to $144 on day one. Raises $3.5B.
2023
All-time revenue record
Revenue reaches $9.9B. 448M nights booked. 7.7M+ active listings. Market cap ~$85B.

The Impact

The disruption by numbers

Secondary data combined with our primary survey paints a clear picture of how Airbnb reshaped the hospitality landscape.

Asset-light model

Airbnb owns zero properties yet manages 7.7M+ listings worldwide - more than the top 5 hotel chains combined. This is the defining characteristic of platform-based disruption.

The network effect

More hosts attract more guests. More guests attract more hosts. This self-reinforcing loop made Airbnb's scale nearly impossible to replicate, creating a durable competitive moat.

RevPAR impact on hotels

A Boston University study found that for every 1% increase in Airbnb supply, hotel revenue per available room (RevPAR) drops by ~0.05%. In high-penetration cities, this compounds to millions in lost revenue annually.

The COVID test

Bookings dropped 72% in March 2020. Yet Airbnb recovered faster than traditional hotels by 2021 - pivoting to isolated home stays and long-term rentals, a flexibility hotels simply couldn't match.
